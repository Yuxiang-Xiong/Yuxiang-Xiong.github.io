Birds or Birders? Evidence from Citizen Birding Records in China

This project studies observation bias in citizen birding records. Using large-scale records from the China Bird Report system, matched with mobility, COVID-risk, holiday, observer-experience, and repeated-visit measures, it asks whether recorded species richness reflects birds or birders. The evidence shows that mobility and observation effort strongly shape checklist counts, observer counts, site coverage, and recorded species. After effort controls enter, the mobility-richness relationship falls sharply. The project argues that economic studies using birding data should model the observation equation before drawing biodiversity conclusions.

Methods / data: China Bird Report records; Amap mobility; COVID-risk variation; event-study and DID diagnostics; occupancy/detection diagnostics.

High-Risk Pesticide Restrictions, Crop Exposure, and Observed Bird Diversity

This paper studies whether China's restrictions on high-risk organophosphate insecticides for vegetable use improved observed bird diversity in counties with greater crop exposure. I build a county-year-bird-group panel for 2013-2019 and estimate a triple-difference design that interacts vegetable exposure, the post-2017 policy period, and sensitive bird groups. Higher vegetable exposure is associated with relative post-policy increases in observed richness, records, and taxon counts for sensitive groups. The interpretation is exposure-matched evidence on pesticide regulation and observed bird diversity, not proof of nationwide bird population recovery.

Methods / data: Bird functional-group panel; SPAM2020 crop exposure; 2017 policy timing; triple difference; event windows.

Trade Policy Exposure and Bird Species Richness: Evidence from U.S. Counties after PNTR

This paper asks whether a national trade-policy shock appears in ecological monitoring data. It links U.S. county-year bird records to county exposure to China's receipt of Permanent Normal Trade Relations and estimates a continuous-treatment difference-in-differences model. The preferred weighted specification finds higher observed bird species richness after 2001 in more exposed counties. The project emphasizes observed richness as the monitoring outcome and studies adjustment margins that may connect trade exposure, local economic activity, environmental pressure, and ecological records.

Methods / data: U.S. county bird records; PNTR exposure; continuous-treatment DID; weighted specifications; heterogeneity analysis.

Local Water Competition and Crop Productivity: Evidence from U.S. Shale Development

Crop production depends on local water availability, and shale development can add a new industrial claim on that water. This paper links U.S. county-crop-year outcomes to FracFocus water disclosures and compares more and less water-intensive crops within county-years. The average national gradient is negative but imprecise. The strongest evidence appears in water-scarce agricultural counties, where shale water demand overlaps more directly with agricultural water needs. The paper documents a localized water-competition margin that broad water-price or output accounts can miss.

Methods / data: FracFocus disclosures; U.S. crop-county-year panel; crop water-intensity gradient; fixed effects; scarcity heterogeneity.

Weather Information and Disaster Death Losses in Africa

Weather information can shape whether climate shocks become human losses. This paper studies disaster death losses in subnational Africa, using rainfall, weather-station histories, and geocoded disaster locations. Districts closer to active weather stations show a smaller increase in death losses after rainfall deficits, especially within 50 kilometers of a station. Recorded disaster occurrence does not fall in the same way, suggesting that reporting and loss severity are different margins. The estimates are interpreted as observational gradients, not treatment effects of station construction.

Methods / data: District-year rainfall shocks; weather-station distance; geocoded disasters; local comparisons; spatial diagnostics.

  • Climate adaptation
  • Disaster risk
  • Africa
  • Information infrastructure

Policy Exposure and Lake Water-Quality Screening: Evidence from U.S. Agricultural Watersheds

This study evaluates whether agricultural trade-policy exposure can provide an auxiliary risk layer for lake water-quality screening. It links a 2008-2020 U.S. lake-year panel of remotely sensed trophic-risk predictions to lake-level exposure to China's retaliatory agricultural tariffs during the U.S.-China trade war. Higher-exposure lakes show larger post-2018 increases in predicted risk, but event-study, placebo, and support diagnostics rule out a strong causal reading. Once historical water quality and watershed predictors enter, tariff exposure adds little forecasting power.

Methods / data: U.S. lake-year panel; remotely sensed water-quality risk; agricultural tariff exposure; event studies; screening validation.

Clean Heating and Household Fuel Substitution: Evidence from Middle-Aged and Older Households in China

This paper evaluates whether northern China's clean-heating pilots appear in household fuel choices among middle-aged and older CHARLS households. Linking CHARLS panel waves to pilot-city exposure, it estimates fixed-effects difference-in-differences models for heating fuel, cooking fuel, respiratory health, and cost outcomes. The clearest evidence appears on the targeted heating margin: pilot exposure is associated with lower reported solid heating fuel, lower any-solid-fuel use, and higher reconstructed clean-heating categories. Health and cost estimates are mixed and imprecise, so the project stays within a mechanism interpretation rather than a full welfare audit.

Methods / data: CHARLS household and individual panel; clean-heating pilot cities; fixed-effects DID; boundary outcomes.

  • Clean heating
  • Household energy
  • Health
  • CHARLS

Regional Spatial Integration and Innovation Network Reorganization: Evidence from City Pairs within Chinese Urban Agglomerations

This project studies whether transport connectivity within urban agglomerations is associated with reorganization of joint-patent collaboration networks. The sample covers 1,461 city pairs inside 19 official Chinese urban agglomerations from 2004 to 2023, combining geocoded patent applicants, high-speed rail connection, agglomeration policy timing, and applicant types. High-speed rail is associated with a more active extensive margin of joint-patent relationships, but PPML count estimates are not stable. Growth is concentrated in a small share of city pairs, especially core-periphery, pre-existing cooperation, and firm-university channels. The interpretation is selective network reorganization, not uniform diffusion.

Methods / data: Chinese invention-patent applicant geocoding; city-pair-year panel; high-speed rail; LPM, PPML, log(1+y); network decomposition.

Which Urban Access Reforms Help Migrants Stay? Evidence from Urban China

China's hukou reforms lowered formal barriers to local registration, but lower thresholds may not create usable settlement pathways for migrants. This paper matches CMDS repeated cross-sections to city-level hukou-threshold measures for 339,819 person-wave observations in 120 destination cities. Overall relaxation after 2014 is not statistically clear, while employment-channel relaxation is more consistently positively associated with retention intention. Home-purchase, talent, and investment channels are less stable. The paper shifts attention from whether hukou reform lowers formal barriers to which access channels can become usable settlement pathways.

Methods / data: CMDS repeated cross-sections; city hukou-threshold measures; city-level policy exposure; fixed effects and association design.

  • Migration

Construction Before Agglomeration: Cross-Boundary Governance and Built-Up Expansion in Shenshan

Cross-boundary development zones may create new agglomeration, but they may also first convert land and infrastructure before market activity follows. This paper studies Shenshan, a Shenzhen-led cooperation zone built from former Haifeng territory. Using an annual one-kilometer grid panel and a staged difference-in-differences design, it compares grids inside Shenshan with nearby untreated grids across the baseline, early-cooperation, and strengthened-governance phases. The clearest post-2017 response is built-up expansion, not synchronized market prosperity in lights, broad POI measures, or land transactions. The project frames Shenshan as construction before agglomeration.

Methods / data: 1 km grid-year panel; staged DID; built-up area; night lights; POI; land transactions; cross-case comparison.

  • Development zones

Green Representation, Trade Barriers, and Chinese Exports

This project studies whether European Green political representation is associated with China-facing market access. It combines China-destination-HS6-year exports with Green election, parliament, cabinet, tariff, and UNCTAD TRAINS non-tariff-measure data. The newer letter version uses a Folke-style Green threshold-crossing design and finds exploratory reduced-form evidence consistent with higher policy barriers and lower exports in a narrow product-level specification. A longer descriptive version studies Green cabinet participation and Chinese exports to Europe. The current interpretation is exploratory, not a strong causal claim about Green cabinet participation.

Methods / data: China HS6 exports; European party, parliament, and cabinet data; tariffs; UNCTAD TRAINS NTMs; threshold and fixed-effect designs.

  • Green parties
  • Trade barriers
  • Chinese exports

Higher-Education Expansion, Maternal Cohorts, and Child Education Spending in China

China's higher-education expansion changed the schooling environment faced by a generation of young adults. This paper asks whether that generation later invested differently in its children and whether additional spending carried visible household costs. Using linked CHFS and CIEFR-HS data, I estimate joint maternal and paternal cohort-window models. Mothers who were ages 18-22 in 1999 are associated with higher child education spending, especially on tutoring, online education, and other nonformal margins. The pattern is strongest in high-income and high-asset households and is not accompanied by lower basic consumption, higher education debt, heavier school burden, or lower maternal work hours.

Methods / data: Linked CHFS and CIEFR-HS data; maternal and paternal cohort-window models; PPML; spending-margin decomposition.

Childhood Parental Absence and Family Insurance Responses to Parental Health Risks

This paper studies whether early parent-child separation weakens adult children's family-insurance response to parents' later-life health risks. It links 2015 and 2020 CHARLS child-parent dyads to childhood histories from the 2014 life-history survey. Parental health deterioration is treated as a care-need shock, and return co-residence is the high-commitment support margin. The preferred specification shows that adult children with childhood parental absence have a weaker co-residence response after parental health deterioration, while money and in-kind support margins show much weaker patterns. A finite-horizon dynamic choice model organizes the evidence as a family-insurance problem.

Methods / data: CHARLS life-history data; 2015/2020 child-parent dyads; health-deterioration response design; dynamic choice model.

  • Elder care
  • Informal insurance
  • CHARLS
